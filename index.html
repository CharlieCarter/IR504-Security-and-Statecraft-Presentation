Background

Now, I’d like to give some background and provide some broader context relating to the substantive focus of the research.

  • ‘Terrorism’ is a contested term.
  • States (and individuals) apply the term to different classes of actors, tactics, events.
  • The term’s contested status has historically precluded the codification of a single, general definition of terrorism into international law.

Questions

First, the claim that terrorism is contested in international politics, specifically, is one that has been advanced by a number of authors, but is this true generally? Can we find evidence that states consistently and generally speak about terrorism in different ways? Are some dimensions of terrorism contested while others are not? Existing research has put forward a number of responses to these questions, but I believe that my methodological approach gets us closer to answering this question definitively and, separately, breaking apart the dimensions of contestation at a very granular level. This means being able to test whether state rhetoric on terrorism varies along theoretically-defined dimensions and, then, testing whether some features of states can explain variation on these dimensions. THEN If we accept that terrorism is contested, what consequences might we actually expect? Do states with different understandings of terrorism cooperate on counterterrorism efforts? Are the domestic counterterrorism policies in two states more dissimilar if they advance wildly different understandings of terrorism in international politics? And, when states change how they use they term terrorism, can we attribute this shift to some other salient change in international politics like foreign aid, newfound membership in international organisations, or broader shifts in the international terrorism discourse? These questions have been relatively less explored, but I believe that identifying what the concrete effects of contestation over terrorism are, if there are any, is truly where the value in this research is.

  • Is terrorism truly contested?
  • What features of states might explain how they understand and use the term ‘terrorism’?
  • Is this contestation ephemeral, or enduring?
  • What are the consequences of terrorism’s contested status? (on policy, behaviour, etc.)

Approach


Is terrorism truly contested?

  • State rhetoric in UN ⮕ reveals latent definition or understanding of terrorism
  • Assess whether meaningful differences are observed

What features of states might explain how they understand and use the term ‘terrorism’?

  • Measure how states each individually use ‘terrorism’ on common scales
  • Ascertain 1. the magnitude of differences in ‘terrorism’ usage across state attributes and 2. assess whether these differences are larger than those expected under chance.

Theory and Expectations

Caveat: democratic states may be more accountable to their publics, and public attitudes may particularly constrain how they talk about terrorism on world stage, but authoritarian states like China may face particular pressure to provide security to their citizens to prevent unrest.

Domestic Politics, Public Opinion, and Threats

  • Selectorate Theory (Bueno de Mesquita et al., 2003) and Foreign Policy Analysis with emphasis on two-level games (Putnam, 1988; Conceição-Heldt and Mello, 2017)
  • Terrorism = security threat to a domestic public
  • Public dissatisfaction with state response to terrorism threatens gov’s survival, especially in democracies

Expectations

  • Democracies = terrorism more salient in rhetoric, international legal solutions sought after
  • Governments with domestic allies which employ political violence against illegitimate targets seek to obfuscate and muddle debate

Theory and Expectations

Emphasis on international competition, constraining one’s adversaries by shaping the counterterrorism discourse, norms, and institutions. Title is probably a little misleading and might be changed, as my thoughts here concern state identities and ideas, but I use the terms ‘strategy’ and ‘interests’ because I see purposive manipulation of the definition of terrorism to be at the forefront of this section.

Framing, Political Rhetoric, and Strategy

  • Critical Terrorism Studies (CTS), Rhetorical Coercion, Strategic Communication
  • ‘Terrorism’ is a label with significant costs
  • Opprobrium and highly negative connotation grant it significant delegitimising power

Expectations

  • State representatives from political parties that endorse political violence seek to obfuscate meaning of ‘terrorism’
  • Formerly colonised states which decolonised violently assert legitimate right to resist domination + draw attention to ‘root causes’ of terrorism
  • Authoritarian or domestically violent governments seek to obfuscate terrorism’s meaning

Data

The United Nations General Debate Corpus contains 8093 English-language transcriptions of speeches delivered during the United Nations General Debates held between 1970 and 2018

The full corpus contains 15,682 complete observations while the reduced sample contains 11,673 complete observations.

More recent years are available but these will require mutiply imputing data, which i have yet to fully integrate into the paper.

Speeches are given by representatives of states, which might range from Foreign Ministers to Heads of State.

Importantly, the UN General Debate offers an annual forum for representatives of UN member-states to appear before their peers and deliver a speech on the issues they deem relevant. My contention is that this set of issues will often include terrorism and, when it does, that the way states talk about terrorism reveals their preferred or dominant understanding of the concept.

  • United Nations General Debate Corpus 1970-2018 (UNGDC) (Jankin Mikhaylov, Baturo, and Dasandi, 2017)
  • One observation = one use of term ‘terrorism’ in a speech by a state representative
  • FastText 1M pretrained word vectors dataset (Mikolov et al., 2018) for embeddings
  • Varieties of Party Identity and Organisation (V-Party) for political party data
  • Varieties of Democracy (V-Dem) for country-year political data (Coppedge et al., 2022; Pemstein et al., 2022)
  • The Maddison Project (Bolt and Luiten Van Zanden, 2020) for real GDP per capita
  • Issue Correlates of War (ICOW) for (de)colonisation data (Hensel and Mitchell, 2007)
  • Global Terrorism Database (GTD) for terrorist attack prevalence (START, 2022)

Method

  • À la Carte Embedding for measurement (Arora et al., 2015; Khodak et al., 2018)

  • conText embedding regression (Rodriguez, Spirling, and B. M. Stewart, 2022) for inference

  • Predict a word-embedding vector for a use of the term ‘terrorism’ given country-year and individual-year attributes + year FE

Embeddings are

  • Quantitative representations of words or word-like tokens

  • generally derived from machine learning models

  • vectors in a Euclidean space which encodes socially-significant linguistic dimensions

Words with similar meanings will occupy similar positions in the semantic space

Resulting Embeddings

  • Each observation assigned a value in a 300-dimensional semantic space and regressed on covariates.
  • Dependent variable: à la carte word embeddings for each use of the word “terrorism”.
  • Multivariate multiple regression framework to predict 300 outcome variables.
  • Standard errors obtained via bootstrapping and significance assessed via permutation.
